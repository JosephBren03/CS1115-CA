Facts

Name & Classification

The scientific name for red pandas is 'Ailurus fulgens', although they have many names such as "firefox" and "red-bear-cat". The name panda is said to come from the Nepali word 'ponya,' which means 'bamboo or plant eating animal'.

Originally, there was thought to be one species of red panda, however there are actually two, Ailurus fulgens and Ailurus fulgens fulgens, which primarily vary in size and weight.

Physical Description & Behaviour

The red panda is slightly larger and heavier than a house cat, with a body shape somewhat similar to a cat crossed with a bear. They are about 2 feet long, with a long, bushy, ringed tail. They have a thick reddish-brown coat with a belly and limbs that are black. There are white markings on the side of their heads and above the eyes. Their heads are large and round, with a short snout and pointed ears. Red pandas also have a soft, wooly undercoat. They have semi-retractable claws to allow them to move between trees easily and to defend themselves, aswell as a wrist bone which acts similarly to a thumb which they use to grasp bamboo to eat.

Red pandas are very skillful and acrobatic animals that tend to stay in trees. They use their tails for balance and to cover themselves in winter for warmth. They are active in early morning and late afternoon. They become far less active in winter, where they only are active to eat. Red pandas are generally solitary creatures but will join in pairs during their mating season.

Habitat & Diet

Almost half of the red panda's habitat is in the Eastern Himalayas where they live in high-altitude temperate forests in India, Nepal, Myanmar, Bhutan and Southern China. Loss of forest and bamboo has caused a steady decline in population. Their reddish-brown coat allows them to blend in with the environment, where fir trees are covered in similarly coloured moss and white lichen.

The red panda's diet is 98% bamboo, although they occasionally eat fruit, insects, small reptiles and various other plantlife. However, they only digest 24% of the bamboo they eat.
