Protections

Anti-Poaching

There exists anti-poaching units and community-based conservation programmes in Nepal which aim to protect and monitor red panda habitats. They achieve this through information boards, radio broadcasting and an annual International Red Panda Day. In Nepal, any person found guilty of killing, buying or selling red panda pelts will face a fine of up to the equivalent of $1,000 USD and potentially up to 10 years in jail.

Zoos

Many zoos, including Dublin Zoo, support the Red Panda Network's Forest Guardian Programme in Nepal, which aims to monitor red panda populations and their natural habitats.

In recent years, zoos have made great progress with red pandas in captivity. By 2015, 42 zoos across Japan kept a total of 219 red pandas. By 2019, 182 zoos in Europe kept 407 red pandas. Additionally, zoos across the Americas and Australia have created various breeding programmes.

Threats

The global red panda population is reported to have declined by 50% in the past 20 years. There may be as few as 2,500 red pandas remaining in the wild as the species continues to decline. The main driving factors in their population decline are habitat loss and poaching.

Habitat Loss

The primary threat to red pandas is the destruction of their natural habitat, due to deforestation and livestock disturbances. Logging damages the environment for bamboo to grow, and livestock damages the growth of bamboo by trampling. Additionally, logging removes trees which red pandas use for nesting. They also face the effects of climate change.

Diseases & Dogs

Free-roaming dogs spread disease, which are deadly to red pandas. These dogs are used in livestock herding and protecting from predators, but they also kill red pandas. These dogs often carry several of a range of gastrointestinal parasites and spread them to red pandas. Many of these viruses have an incredibly high mortality rate in red pandas.

Poaching

Red pandas are poached for their pelts and tails, mostly in China and Myanmar. Red panda skins have been smuggled across the China-Myanmar border in more recent years. Often their pelts are fashioned into fur caps which are sold in markets. Red pandas are also often killed when they are caught in traps which are supposed to capture other wild animals.
